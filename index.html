Scientific Advancement


The scientific advancements of the modern age, spanning from the late 19th century to the present, represent a remarkable era of innovation and discovery that has profoundly shaped the way we understand and interact with the world.


  1. John Gorrie's Machanical Ice Machine, 1851
    2.

    • John Gorrie was the first inventor to secure a U.S. patent for artificial refrigeration.

    • Gorrie was not the first scientist to research refrigeration, nor was he the last.

    • His invention paved the way for the development of commercial and personal refrigeration systems.

    • Gorrie’s work significantly changed how humans transport, store, and purchase food, with a profound and lasting impact on society.

  2. ENIAC, 1946
    3.

    • Today's computers are compact, often fitting in your pocket, but the ENIAC was the size of an entire room.

    • The ENIAC was built to solve large numerical problems. And it weighed 80 tons and occupied 1,800 square feet.

    • ENIAC is recognized as one of the world’s first modern computers, a precursor to the machines that are essential to modern civilization.

    • ENIAC is also considered one of the first data centers, a technology now crucial to nearly every industry.



The Industrial Revolution


The Industrial Revolution, a transformative period spanning from the late 18th century to the mid-19th century, reshaped the fabric of human existence. It was a time of seismic shifts, where innovation collided with tradition, and the world was propelled into an era of mechanization, urbanization, and societal upheaval.

  1. Origins and Innovations

    • Started in the late 18th century in Britain.
    • Introduction of machines like the Spinning Jenny and the steam engine revolutionized industries.

  2. Urbanization and Social Impact

    • Rapid urbanization as people moved to cities for factory work.
    • Overcrowding, poor working conditions, and pollution became prominent issues.

  3. Global Expansion

    • Industrial practices spread to colonies and other parts of the world.
    • Growth of global trade networks and increased interconnectedness.

  4. Economic and Social Changes

    • Massive increase in production and efficiency.
    • Significant changes in society, including labor conditions and urban life.

  5. Legacy and Reflection

    • The Industrial Revolution set the stage for the modern world with its technological and economic advancements.
    • Highlights the need for balancing innovation with human welfare.
